# Cast Portraits

Physical descriptions for AI image generation. Mirrors `cast.md` § numbering — pick a character, paste the paragraph into your generator, edit lightly for your tool's grammar.

**Tone reminders before you generate:**
- Empathetic realism, not caricature. Mood reference: *Disco Elysium*, *Pathologic*. Warm lighting, grain, lived-in faces. No glossy / heroic / AAA-character renders.
- Witnesses on field service wear specific things: clean button-down + tie + dress slacks (men) or modest skirt-and-blouse / dress below the knee (women). Closed-toe shoes broken in. Small canvas or leather shoulder bag for magazines.
- Householders look like whoever they were when the doorbell rang on a Saturday morning. They're not posing.
- All adults; no minors in this v1 cast.
- Default-American suburban setting unless otherwise noted. Specific ethnic identity only when called out in `cast.md` (e.g. § 6.0.3 Jewish, § 6.0.2 Catholic via "St. Mark's" parish — the cultural specificity is canon and matters).
- The **empathy bar** applies to art the same as to dialogue. Read `cast.md` § 6.0.3 / 6.0.4 / 6.6 red lines before generating those characters.

When commissioning art, pair the portrait paragraph with the relevant `cast.md` section so the illustrator sees the voice profile alongside the look.

## § 2. Player Character

22 years old, just baptized — fresh into adult responsibility inside the Truth. Slim, average height, the slight wiriness of someone who walks territories on Saturdays and doesn't lift. Gender flexible (player choice); both presentations are clean-cut, modest, deliberately unremarkable. Hair short and neatly combed for men, shoulder-length or shorter for women, pulled back simply. Eyes wary in a way the face hasn't caught up to — the trained smile is present but the eyes don't fully commit. Clothes: a button-down shirt, plain tie loosened just slightly, dark dress slacks; or a knee-length skirt with a cardigan over a blouse. Shoes practical, well-cared-for, scuffed at the toe. A small canvas messenger bag at the hip with magazines visible at the top.

*Setting:* on a suburban porch, mid-morning, holding a copy of *The Lighthouse* face-out. Backlit by autumn sun. Body language slightly forward, polite, ready to be told no.

*Avoid:* runway-attractive renders; "fresh-faced cult member" caricature; doe-eyed innocence.

## § 3. Family

### § 3.1 The Parent in the Truth

Mid-50s, the parent the player most resembles (player chooses mother or father at character creation). Reading glasses pushed up on the head or in a soft case in the breast pocket. The face is warm and lined in a way that says *they have prayed about you*. Hair greying at the temples — kept; not dyed. For the mother: hair set or in a simple style, no flash. For the father: clean-shaven or short-trimmed beard, conservative cut. Eyes attentive, watchful in the way of someone who watches *for love* — the watching is the love. Clothes: at home, soft and modest — a buttoned cardigan, a long skirt or a polo with khakis. A Bible or *Lighthouse* nearby on every surface. A small cross-stitched scripture in a frame somewhere in the background.

*Setting:* a tidy kitchen mid-morning, coffee in two mugs, the player's empty chair across the table. Or a sofa with the *Songs of the Truth* hymnal open between them.

*Avoid:* glaring / cold / sinister framing. They love. The love is the trap.

---

### § 3.2 The Sibling Drifting

Mid-20s, same household generation as the player. The clothes are the first tell — small things outside the Witness aesthetic: a band t-shirt under a hoodie, a piercing the parent didn't notice yet, hair a little long or freshly cut in a style that wasn't pre-cleared. Build similar to the player's — they grew up in the same kitchen. Eyes that look down or away when meetings come up; the eye contact slips. A phone in their hand constantly, screen tilted so others can't see. Face slightly tired in a way that isn't physical — the social distance is the tired.

*Setting:* on the back porch alone, smoking maybe (the parent doesn't know), or hunched over their phone late at night with the player. A bedroom door half-closed.

*Avoid:* "punk rebellion" stylization; the apostate-leather-jacket trope; anything that signals they've already left. They haven't. They're drifting.

---

### § 3.3 The Grandparent

Late 70s or 80s. Soft-faced, slow-moving, hands that don't quite work the way they used to. Hair white or thinning, kept simply. Skin paper-soft, with the spots of long sun and long life. The eyes are the centerpiece: clear, kind, slightly far-off — they're listening but they're also remembering. Body small, settled into a chair. A blanket over the lap or shoulders even when it isn't cold. Clothes plain and clean — a cardigan, a worn collared shirt, slippers. A wedding ring still on the finger though the spouse may be gone.

*Setting:* a deep armchair in a sitting room with old photographs in mismatched frames. A *Lighthouse* on the side table next to a tissue box. Light through gauzy curtains.

*Avoid:* "wise sage" framing; the "secret apostate grandparent" knowing-look trope. They aren't secretly anything. They notice — that's all.

## § 4. Congregation

### § 4.1 The Coordinator of Elders — Brother Phillips

Late 50s, well-kept, a man who has been on the platform for thirty years. Average build, slightly soft at the middle in the way of a man who eats well at after-meeting socials. Hair greying at the temples, conservatively cut, neat. Clean-shaven or with a tidy mustache. The face is *kind* — the kindness is the first thing and the last thing. Eyes warm, attentive, with the slight wateriness of someone who has watched many people leave and many people stay. Suit: charcoal or navy two-button, white or pale-blue shirt, conservative tie (small repeated pattern, gold or burgundy). Polished shoes. A wedding band. A *Lighthouse* tucked under the arm or in his hand at all times.

*Setting:* the Hall lobby after a meeting, mid-handshake. Or a folding chair pulled close, leaning in for an "encouragement" session. Soft fluorescent light.

*Avoid:* sinister framing, smug framing, condescension on the face. He is genuinely kind. That's exactly what makes him dangerous.

---

### § 4.2 The Strict Elder — Brother Whitcomb

Mid-40s to early 50s, the man you don't want to catch your eye in the lobby. Built compact, tense across the shoulders. Hair short, severe part, no nonsense. A small permanent furrow between the brows. Clean-shaven, jawline tight. Eyes direct in a way that doesn't waver — the discomfort the eye contact produces is the *point*. Suit fits crisply, no softness — black or dark grey, white shirt, dark plain tie knotted small and tight. A clipboard or a notebook in his hand more often than not. The body language stays at *attention* even in casual conversation.

*Setting:* the lobby after the meeting, intercepting someone near the literature counter. Or seated across a folding table in the back room, *Lighthouse* open between them, the page underlined.

*Avoid:* cartoon villain framing, sneer, glare. He is not cruel. He believes he is being faithful. The pressure is the *system* operating through him.

---

### § 4.3 The Sister Pioneer

Early 20s, the age range of the player. Beautiful in a wholesome way — the beauty is *unflashy*; she would be uncomfortable if you called it out. Hair shoulder-length or longer, simply kept, often pulled half-back. Skin clear, light makeup if any. The smile arrives easily and stays. Eyes bright, sincere, with the slight earnestness of someone who hasn't yet had to defend her faith from the inside. Dress: a knee-length floral or solid-color dress with sleeves, a cardigan in case the Hall is cold; or modest skirt-and-blouse for service. A name badge clipped at the collar when pioneering. A magazine bag.

*Setting:* on the sidewalk between houses, mid-stride, the morning sun catching her hair. Or at the back of the Hall lobby with a coffee cup, talking to two other sisters.

*Avoid:* romance-novel-cover styling; "model publisher" rendered as a glossy character; perfection without humanity. She is a person.

---

### § 4.4 The Service Partner (recurring)

Mid- to late 20s, the player's age range. Built like a real human — average, slightly soft, broken-in. Hair functional, beard scruff if male (not pioneering, doesn't have to be clean-shaven every day). The clothes are *almost* the Witness aesthetic but slightly more relaxed — a polo and chinos rather than a full suit and tie on a Saturday service day, even though some sticklers would push back. The face is the most *honest* face in the cast — they laugh easily, they look tired when they're tired, their eyes don't hide things the way the elders' do. A travel mug of coffee in one hand more often than not.

*Setting:* in the car between territories, music low, mid-conversation. Or on the sidewalk waiting while the player knocks. The shoulder bag with magazines slung casually.

*Avoid:* "comic relief" framing; making them a sidekick. They are the player's most honest mirror in the cast.

---

### § 4.5 The Sister Who Talks

Mid-40s to mid-50s, the social hub. Built warmly — soft, mid-weight, comfortable. Hair styled with care for the meeting day, slightly bouffant or set in a way that says she went to the salon Friday. A scarf or statement jewelry — modest but *visible*. Lipstick. The face is *open*, eyes bright and attentive in a way that pulls people toward her at the back of the Hall. The smile lands fast and lands real. Dress modest but with personality — a printed blouse, a long skirt, an embroidered cardigan. A small handbag with magazines and tissues and a notebook for prayer requests.

*Setting:* the Hall lobby after the meeting, mid-conversation with two sisters, one hand on someone's arm. Coffee in the other hand. Half a dozen people waiting their turn to talk to her.

*Avoid:* gossip-villain framing; mean-girl rendering. She thinks of herself as a *connector*. The damage she does is sincere.

## § 5. Outside

### § 5.1 The Worldly Coworker

Mid- to late 20s, the player's age. Dressed for the workplace they share with the player — business casual, no tie if the player's job is white-collar; uniform polo or apron if it's retail / food service. The clothes carry small flags the player would notice: a tattoo at the wrist or behind the ear, a piercing, hair dyed or cut a way no publisher cuts it. The face is *open* and *uncoached* — they have not been trained to perform anything. A coffee in one hand, a phone in the other.

*Setting:* this character never appears in person on-screen per cast.md § 5.1 — they exist as a phone screen, a text bubble, a voicemail thumbnail. Generate the portrait *as their messaging-app avatar* or *as a candid photo they sent the player*: at a coworker's birthday, at a concert, at their kitchen counter at 11pm. Casual. Not posed.

*Avoid:* "secular saviour" framing; "free-spirit who shows the player the light"; overt counterculture stylization. They are just a normal person who happens to live outside the player's world.

### § 5.2 The Householder of Note

Cast.md § 5.2 ships three archetype variants. Commission one *or* all three — the player implicitly picks via which house they keep returning to.

**Margaret** — late 60s, widowed, lives alone. Build small, slightly stooped at the shoulders. Hair short, white or silver, simply set. Glasses on a chain. Skin softly lined; the face carries grief gracefully — it has lived there for two years. Eyes warm but slightly far away. Cardigan over a modest blouse and a long skirt; orthopedic shoes broken in. A wedding ring still on the finger though the husband is gone. *Setting:* her doorway opens fully — she's pleased to see the publishers. A spotless tidy living room visible behind her, framed photographs on every surface.

**Daniel** — early 30s, intellectual. Lean, slightly soft from desk work. Hair shaggy-tidy, neither styled nor neglected. Glasses, often the kind with thin metal frames. A short beard or two-day stubble. The face *thinks* before it speaks — the eyes hold yours and then look down to consider. Wears a worn cardigan or sweater over a t-shirt, jeans, sock-feet in the doorway. A book in his hand more often than not, finger holding the place. *Setting:* the door opens halfway, then all the way once he realizes he wants to talk. A bookshelf visible in the hallway behind him.

**Sarah & Tom** — couple, mid-40s, parents to a child with a medical condition. Both built normal and slightly tired. Tom: clean-cut, short hair, jeans and a flannel shirt on weekends. Sarah: longer hair pulled back, no makeup on a Saturday morning, a hand-me-down sweatshirt with school logo. The eyes carry exhaustion that isn't quite hidden. A child's toys visible just inside the doorway. The wedding bands are slightly worn down. *Setting:* one answers the door; the other is audible from inside — calling about the kid, asking who it is. The interaction happens against the sound of family life.

*Avoid (all variants):* archetypal staging. They must feel specific. Margaret is *this particular widow*, not "the lonely widow." Daniel is *this particular intellectual*, not "the polite atheist." Sarah & Tom are *these particular parents*, not "the family in crisis."

## § 6. Householder Archetypes (Maple Street)

The door-knock characters the player meets across the twelve houses of Maple Street.

### § 6.0.1 Polite Refuser — House #1 (Atheist, tired flavor)

Mid-40s, a man tired in a Saturday-morning way that isn't about religion. Average build, soft at the middle. Hair short, going grey at the temples, slightly rumpled from sleep or from the back of the couch. Two-day stubble, not by design — by inattention. Eyes friendly but glassy, mid-something when the doorbell rang. The clothes are pajama-adjacent: a faded t-shirt of a band or a 5K he ran in 2019, sweatpants or worn jeans, no shoes. Maybe a baby on the hip or a phone in the hand. He has had publishers at the door multiple times this year and the *please-just-go-away* is real but soft.

*Setting:* the door opens partway, kitchen sounds behind him, a TV audible in another room.

*Avoid:* sneering / smug-atheist framing. He doesn't have an argument; he has laundry.

---

### § 6.0.1 Polite Refuser — House #12 (Atheist, intellectual flavor)

Late 30s to mid-40s, gender flexible — commission whichever fits the house art best. Built ordinarily, slightly soft from desk work. Hair conventionally kept, slightly grown-out. Glasses with thin frames. The face is *considered* — they pause before they speak, even at the door. Eyes attentive in a *thoughtful* way, not in a *suspicious* way. Wears something a step nicer than House #1 — a thin sweater over a button-down, fitted jeans, socks no shoes. A coffee mug in hand and a book or magazine just inside on the entryway table — the publishers will register that the book on top is a serious one without being able to read the title.

*Setting:* the door opens partway. A bookshelf glimpsed in the next room. NPR or a podcast audible faintly behind them.

*Avoid:* arrogance, condescension toward the publisher, or any "smarter-than-you" framing. They are polite to the publisher *as people*; they reject the offer with words rather than dismissal.

---

### § 6.0.2 Polite Refuser — House #5 (Catholic, M3 canonical)

Late 40s to mid-50s, the lapsed-Catholic-with-the-parish-card variety. Could be a man or woman; commission either. Built ordinarily, slightly softer than they were in their 30s. Hair conventionally cut and slightly faded in color. Skin with the small markers of long suburban life — sun damage at the forearms, a watch worn for years. The face is *familiar* in the way of someone who has grown up Catholic and lived next to a JW for twenty years and knows exactly how this conversation goes. Eyes friendly but already declining. Wears whatever weekend clothes the household defaults to — a tucked polo, jeans, a Saturday sweatshirt. A small cross necklace or a Marian medal sometimes visible.

*Setting:* the door opens partway. A small framed image of a saint or Mary visible on the wall just inside.

*Avoid:* heavy Catholic iconography on the body itself — the religion is community-anchored ("St. Mark's"), not ritual-on-display. One small medal at most.

### § 6.0.3 Polite Refuser — House #3 (Jewish woman)

**Empathy-bar red lines per cast.md § 6.0.3 apply to this image too — read those before generating.** No overt religious signaling on her person. The cultural identity is integrated into normal suburban life — present in the *space*, not paraded on the body.

Mid-50s, woman, suburban professional dressed casually for a Saturday. Built normally, average height. Hair shoulder-length, brown going lightly grey, neatly kept — possibly pulled back. Glasses, the kind chosen for the face. The face is *warm but firm* — the brevity-as-weight cast.md describes is visible in the resting expression: she is friendly *until she isn't*, and the line is clean. Clothes practical, modest, not religious — jeans or slacks, a quarter-zip pullover or a long-sleeved blouse. No visible religious jewelry. The only canonical religious marker is environmental: **a small mezuzah on the doorframe at eye-height**, present in the frame of the portrait.

*Setting:* the doorway open partway. The mezuzah in the frame at her shoulder. The interior of the house visible briefly behind her — a normal suburban living room.

*Avoid:* ANY visual reference that gestures at WWII / Holocaust history. No Star of David necklace visible. No Hebrew text in the frame. No "stage-Jewish" facial styling. She is a person who happens to be Jewish; the dialogue and the visual both refuse to make her *that*.

### § 6.0.4 Polite Refuser — House #8 (gay couple)

**Empathy-bar red lines per cast.md § 6.0.4 apply to this image too — read those before generating.** The couple is not a vehicle for any argument. Render the *door interaction*, not a "couple portrait." Only one partner is at the door; the other is implied off-screen.

The partner at the door: 30s to early 50s, casual Saturday-morning dress, polo or t-shirt, jeans, no shoes. Built ordinarily — they look like the rest of suburban America because they *are* the rest of suburban America. Hair cut conventionally. The face is *politely already finishing this conversation* — kind, but with the same brevity Jewish #3 has. Eyes friendly, not warm. A wedding band on the relevant hand.

*Setting:* the door open partway. The interior visible enough to suggest a lived-in home — two pairs of shoes by the door, a coffee table with two mugs. A second voice is *audible but absent from the frame* — a partner calling from another room about something mundane (dinner, the dog, the TV).

*Avoid:* rainbow flag in the frame. Pride paraphernalia. Any visual "tell" that this is a gay couple beyond the two-mugs / two-shoes / off-screen-voice texture. The whole point per cast.md § 6.0.4 is that they don't announce themselves; the publisher figures it out from the small details.

### § 6.0.5 Polite Refuser — House #10 (Episcopalian)

Late 50s to early 60s, mainline-Protestant-quiet-confidence. Could be a man or woman; commission either. Hair conventionally cut, going silver. The face *settled*, gently lined, no urgency. Skin clear. Eyes warm in a *generous neighborly* way — the warmth is real, but the door is also closing. Clothes Saturday-suburban with a slight tilt toward the put-together: a button-down tucked into chinos, or a long cardigan over a knit top and ankle-length pants. Conservative leather shoes. A book or coffee mug in hand.

*Setting:* the door open partway. Behind them a glimpse of a tidy home — a small painted icon or a framed Psalm calligraphy on the wall, easy to miss.

*Avoid:* heavy Anglican imagery (no biretta, no clerical collar — they're a parishioner, not the priest). The faith is integrated and quiet.

### § 6.1 Hostile Slammer

Could be anyone — age 25 to 65, gender flexible. Build whatever. The portrait isn't about the *person* — it's about *5–15 seconds at the doorway*. The face seen briefly: alert recognition, then closing. The eyes do not soften. The mouth either says one short word or says nothing. A hand on the inside doorknob is the most-rendered detail.

*Setting:* the door cracked open, two inches of interior visible behind them — a wedge of hallway, a TV audible, maybe a dog barking. The door already moving closed.

*Avoid:* anger-as-feature. The slammer isn't *angry* — they are *done*. The interaction is over before they got to anger.

---

### § 6.2.1 Curious Seeker — House #4 (grief, M4.2 canonical)

A woman in her 30s or early 40s. Build ordinary, slightly tired in posture. Hair shoulder-length, pulled back loosely — the kind of pulled-back that happened in passing, not for the day. Some makeup from yesterday under the eyes that hasn't fully come off. The face is *half-distracted* — her brain is still in the conversation she was having with herself when the doorbell rang. Eyes carry something the body hasn't named yet. Wears a long cardigan over a t-shirt and leggings or jeans, socks no shoes. A phone in her hand, the screen recently dark.

*Setting:* the door opens fully — she steps onto the porch, doesn't quite know why she's letting them keep talking. Mid-morning light. A coffee mug abandoned on the porch railing or the kitchen counter visible behind her.

*Avoid:* theatrical grief — red eyes, fresh tears, a tissue clutched in the hand. The grief is in the *texture*, not the volume. Cast.md § 6.2.1: she is *carrying* the grief, not displaying it.

### § 6.2.2 Curious Seeker — House #9 (inquisitive)

A neighbor in their 30s to early 50s, gender flexible. Built ordinarily, weekend-casual dress — jeans and a fleece pullover, or shorts and a t-shirt if the season allows. Hair conventionally cut, slightly windblown. Face *open* and *warm* — the kind of face that doesn't think twice about chatting with a stranger on the porch. Eyes curious in a *neighborly* way, not in a *searching* way. A mug or a tool or a phone in one hand.

*Setting:* the door fully open; they have stepped onto the porch, one hand on the doorframe casually. A garden bed visible at the foot of the porch with weeds half-pulled. They have all morning and no agenda.

*Avoid:* the "polite seeker about to convert" framing. They are *not* searching. Curiosity is the whole thing.

### § 6.6 The Apostate (House #7 trio)

House #7 sub-rolls between Hostile (40%), Wounded (35%), and Gentle (25%) on each knock. **All three are the same person at different moments — same house, same doorframe, same memory of leaving** — but the variants are distinct enough that the player can meet one and then meet another the next visit and read them as different presentations of someone who has lived this story. Commission three separate portraits; same actor / same demographic if you want them to feel like one continuous person, or three distinct people if you want them to feel like neighbors who share the same ex-member status.

Default demographic across all three (canon-flexible): woman, 40s. Suburban. The cost of having left the org is *visible* in small textural details — the apartment hasn't been a couple's apartment in years, the photos on the wall have gaps, the visible mail is single-name. None of this is in the foreground. It's in the *background* of the doorway scene.

#### § 6.6.1 The Hostile One (anger-driven, sister-loss)

Mid-40s. Hair pulled back tightly or cut short, practical. The face *prepared* — she heard the doorbell and was at the door before the second knock. Eyes direct, no warmth, no cruelty either — she is *done* with this conversation before it starts. Mouth set. Posture half-turned already, hand on the inside of the doorframe. Wears something practical: jeans, a fitted sweater or t-shirt. No jewelry except possibly a single ring that isn't a wedding band.

*Setting:* the door open partway. Behind her a hallway. A photo on the wall behind her that includes a younger version of her with another person (a sister) — the photo is at the edge of the frame, ambient, the publishers wouldn't notice it but the player might.

*Avoid:* angry-snarl framing. The anger is *under* the surface — the face is *contained*, the rage measured. She has rehearsed this.

#### § 6.6.2 The Wounded One (grief-driven, mother-loss, M4.3 canonical)

Mid-40s. Hair down, slightly unkempt. The face *tired in a way that isn't about today* — the tiredness has been the resting state for almost five years. Eyes glassy but not actively crying; the grief is *named*, not active. Mouth softer, looser. Posture defeated-not-broken — still in the doorway, doesn't close it. Wears a loose sweater over leggings or pajama pants — Saturday morning, no agenda. A coffee mug in hand or on the threshold.

*Setting:* the door open fully. Behind her a hallway with a single small framed photograph — older woman (mother) — visible without being centered. The room beyond looks lived-in and slightly under-cared-for.

*Avoid:* theatrical sadness. No tears in the frame. The grief is in the *posture* and the *tiredness*, not the face.

#### § 6.6.3 The Gentle One (most-dangerous-archetype, congregation-loss)

Mid-40s. Hair down or loosely tied back. The face *warm and present* — the years of processing are visible as *calm* rather than as exhaustion. Eyes *kind* in a way that lands as choice, not as default — she has decided to be kind to whoever shows up on her porch today. Mouth slightly upward, easy. Posture *relaxed but rooted* — she stands in the doorway like she could stand there an hour. Wears soft house clothes: a long cardigan over a tank top, leggings, slippers. A mug of tea, not coffee.

*Setting:* the door open fully. Behind her, the entryway is tidy and warm. A potted plant. A bookshelf glimpsed in the next room. The space reads as *fully a life*, fully built, fully alone.

*Avoid:* serene-guru framing. The warmth is a *choice she is making in this specific moment*, not an unconditional disposition. She is not happier than the publisher. She carries her loss visibly to anyone who knows what they're looking at.
