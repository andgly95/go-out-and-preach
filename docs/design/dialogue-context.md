# Dialogue Context — Go Out and Preach

> Reference document for any agent generating dialogue content for this game. Read this in full before drafting any line.

This is the project's voice bible. Systems agents don't need it. Dialogue agents must read it before writing anything, including stub placeholders.

---

## 1. Purpose

This document captures the specific texture of how members of a high-control millenarian Christian sect — and the people they speak to at doors — actually talk. Authenticity in this game is a system property: it comes from accumulated specifics, not from any single line. Get the cadence right and the player feels the world. Get it wrong and the whole project reads as outsider fiction.

The fictional organization is **The Society of the Truth**. The texture is drawn from the lived experience of the real-world organization the Society echoes (the Jehovah's Witnesses), but no real names, publications, songs, or living leaders appear in dialogue.

## 3. Generic JW-Tradition Vocabulary (use freely — not trademarked)

These terms are common across the broader religious-studies and ex-member literature and are safe to use directly:

- **Publisher** — a baptized member who participates in ministry.
- **Unbaptized publisher** — teen or convert before baptism who participates in ministry.
- **Pioneer** — volunteer with elevated ministry hours commitment.
  - **Auxiliary pioneer** — temporary, 30 hr/mo
  - **Regular pioneer** — ongoing, 50 hr/mo (numbers may shift in real org over time; pick a number and stick to it in-game)
- **Field service** — the door-to-door ministry activity.
- **Service group** — congregation subdivision based on geography.
- **Service overseer** — elder who coordinates the group.
- **Householder** — the person spoken to at a door. (Org's own term, used in members' reports.)
- **Return visit** — a follow-up to a previously interested householder.
- **Bible study** — an ongoing teaching session with a non-member, usually weekly, in their home.
- **Tract** — a small printed handout.
- **Magazine offer** — the standard door-to-door pitch.
- **Theocratic** — adjective referring to organizational order. "Theocratic warfare," "theocratic arrangement."
- **Worldly** — anything or anyone outside the religion. Used neutrally by members but tinged with separation.
- **Brothers and sisters** — form of address within the congregation. "Brother Henderson," "Sister Marin."
- **The friends** — informal term for fellow members. "The friends are gathering at the park Saturday."
- **In the Truth** / **born-in** — raised in the religion vs. converted.
- **Disfellowshipped** — formally excommunicated. Members must shun.
- **Disassociated** — voluntarily resigned. Treated identically to disfellowshipped.
- **Apostate** — former member who actively opposes. The most charged category.
- **Marked** — informal sanction; not announced but socially enforced.
- **Encouraging** / **encouragement** — gentle correction, peer-level.
- **Counsel** — formal correction from an elder. Heavier than encouragement.
- **Spiritual food** — sermons, magazines, study material.
- **The faithful slave** / **faithful and discreet slave** — the org's claimed mediating role between God and members. Members reference it constantly.
- **Spiritual paradise** — the congregation as a community, contrasted with the broken outside world.

## 4. Theological Framing (what members actually believe)

Dialogue agents do not need to teach theology to the player. They do need to know what characters believe so the speech doesn't ring false. Brief summary:

- **Millenarian** — the current world is in its final days. The end could come any time. Members live in expectation.
- **Restorationist** — true Christianity was lost after the apostles; the Society has restored it.
- **Anti-Trinitarian** — Jesus is God's son, not God. The Holy Spirit is God's active force, not a person.
- **No immortal soul** — the soul is the person; death is unconsciousness; resurrection brings life back. No hellfire.
- **Two-class system** — 144,000 anointed who rule with Christ in heaven; everyone else lives on a paradise earth.
- **Blood doctrine** — abstain from blood, interpreted to prohibit blood transfusions. A signature distinctive.
- **No holidays** — birthdays, Christmas, Easter, Halloween, national holidays. Considered pagan or worldly.
- **Political neutrality** — no voting, no military service, no flag salute, no national anthem.
- **Disfellowshipping and shunning** — formal discipline for unrepentant sin; family and friends cut contact.
- **Imminent end** — Armageddon is close. Members organize their lives around this expectation. The phrase "in this system" implies "before the end."

Members deploy specific apologetic talking points on demand: the Trinity isn't biblical, hellfire isn't biblical, the cross is a pagan symbol, "Jehovah" is God's personal name, the soul dies with the body. These talking points have a rehearsed quality. They have been rehearsed.

## 5. Speech Patterns and Tells

### General cadence

- **Earnest formality.** Even casual conversation between members carries a slight uplift, as if everyone is always halfway to giving a talk.
- **Scripture as punctuation.** Members reference Bible verses in conversation the way other people reference movies. "As Paul says at 1 Corinthians 7:29, the time left is reduced."
- **Stock phrases used unselfconsciously:** "the truth," "the world," "Jehovah's organization," "spiritual food," "the friends," "encouraging," "upbuilding," "the brothers."
- **Hopeful framing of bad things.** Sickness, death, hardship are referenced with immediate redirect to the New System. "Of course, in the New System, none of this will be remembered."
- **"We" frequency.** Members talk as part of a collective. "We don't celebrate birthdays." "We've been studying this in the magazine."
- **Theocratic passive voice.** Decisions from the elder body or up are described without naming agents. "It was decided that..." "It was felt that you might benefit from..."

### Avoided words and concepts

- Members rarely say "religion" positively, even about their own faith — they distinguish "the Truth" from "religion."
- They avoid casual swearing, vulgarity, and slang.
- They avoid "good luck" (substitute: "I hope it goes well"), "Merry Christmas," "Happy Birthday."
- They avoid "amen" outside of structured prayer.
- "Church" is the wrong word for the Hall of Witness. Members will correct it gently if a householder uses it.

### Names and address

- Inside the congregation: "Brother [Lastname]" or "Sister [Lastname]" in formal contexts; first names among peers and close friends.
- Children address adults by Brother/Sister + first name.
- Elders are sometimes referred to in shorthand: "Brother Phillips called me in."
- The Council of Stewards is referenced reverentially but indirectly. Members rarely name individuals.

### Door-to-door cadence

Standard opener variations:
- "Good morning. We're sharing a brief encouraging message from the Bible today."
- "Hi, we're in the neighborhood today talking about [topic]. Have you ever wondered why..."
- "Good morning, we're not asking for anything — we just wanted to leave you with this. (Offers magazine.)"

The presentation is trained to be **under thirty seconds** to the magazine offer or scripture introduction. Members are taught to be brief, courteous, and to leave on a positive note.

After a refusal:
- "Of course, we respect that. May we leave this with you anyway?"
- "Thank you for your time."
- "Take care now."
- "Hope to see you again."

Note: Publishers do *not* default to "have a blessed day." That phrase is generic American Evangelical / Pentecostal / Southern Christian — not characteristically Witness. Witness closings at the door are pragmatic and brief: "take care," "hope to see you again," "we'll let you go," "thank you for your time." The reservedness is the tell.

After interest:
- "Would it be all right if I came back later this week to share more? What's a good time?"
- "I'd love to discuss this further. Would Tuesday or Thursday work?"

## 6. Householder Archetype Voices

For M3, the only one that matters is the Polite Refuser. Sketches for others are included for later milestones.

### Polite Refuser (M3 implementation target)

**Profile:** Suburban American, age 35-65, middle class, vague Christian or post-Christian background. Has had JWs at the door before. No interest in religious conversation but unwilling to be rude. Their goal is to end the interaction as quickly as possible without seeming like a bad person.

**Voice:**
- Slightly awkward warmth. Smiles, but a constrained smile.
- Opens the door partway. Body language says "I'm not going to invite you in."
- Sentences trail off. Uses "uh" and "you know" more in this interaction than in normal conversation.
- Reaches for stock politeness phrases: "I appreciate it, but..." "Thank you, that's kind, but..." "No, I'm — I'm all set, thanks."

**Common defenses (in order of preference):**
1. **Existing belief claim.** "I'm Catholic, actually." / "I have my own beliefs." / "I'm spiritual but not religious."
2. **Time excuse.** "I'm in the middle of something." / "I'd love to chat but I'm running out the door."
3. **Vague refusal.** "I'm not interested but thank you."
4. **Acceptance to end the interaction.** Takes the tract specifically so the conversation will end.

**What they will not do:**
- Engage on theology
- Slam the door (that's the Hostile Slammer)
- Argue (that's the Hostile Christian)
- Ask follow-up questions (that's the Curious Seeker)

**What lands wrong:**
- A Polite Refuser who is too articulate about their reasons. They are not preparing an argument; they are trying to end an interaction.
- A Polite Refuser who is hostile. They are not.
- A Polite Refuser who is interested. They are not.

**Sample interaction (annotated):**

```
PUBLISHER: Good morning. We're sharing an encouraging message from the
           Bible this morning. (Offers magazine.) Have you ever wondered
           why there's so much suffering in the world?

HOUSEHOLDER: Oh — uh, no, I'm good. Thanks though. I have my own beliefs.

PUBLISHER: Of course. We just wanted to leave this with you — it talks
           about that question and a few others. Could I leave one with you?

HOUSEHOLDER: Sure, yeah, that's — that's fine. Thank you.

PUBLISHER: Thank you. Take care now.

HOUSEHOLDER: You too. (Closes door before publisher fully turns.)
```

Note: the householder accepts the magazine specifically to end the interaction. The publisher records this as TRACT_LEFT, a small positive outcome. Neither person changes. This is the most common door interaction in the real world.

### Other archetypes (sketches — implementation in later milestones)

**Hostile Slammer.** Opens door, sees publishers, closes door. Maybe says "no" or "not interested" first. The interaction is 5-15 seconds. The voice is curt, no warmth.

**Curious Seeker.** Asks an actual question. "What do you guys believe about [X]?" Tone is genuinely interested, not testing. The danger for the publisher: this is a real opening, and they will be tempted to oversell. Sample bait: "I've been wondering what happens after we die."

**Lonely Elderly.** Will talk about anything. Doesn't matter what topic — they want company. May invite the publishers in. Voice meanders. Conversation lasts 20+ minutes if allowed. Real-world publishers learn to gently exit.

**Disillusioned Catholic.** Specifically Catholic (or formerly so) and has unresolved questions about church teaching. Voice carries real spiritual searching. This is the archetype most likely to convert in-game.

**Hostile Christian.** Knows enough theology to argue. Often Trinitarian or Evangelical. Wants to debate, sometimes aggressively. "You guys aren't even Christians" energy. Voice is confident, sometimes patronizing.

**The Apostate.** A former member. Will say something the publishers recognize as inside knowledge — a phrase, a critique, a name. Voice carries weight the publishers immediately register. **The doubt meter increments here even on a positive outcome.** Sample tell: "I used to be one of you. Don't waste your time on me." Or, dangerously gentle: "Are you doing okay?"

**The Householder of Note.** Cannot be archetyped in advance — they are written individually. The recurring NPC across the territory.

## 7. Publisher Voices

The player character speaks too. Their lines should reflect:

- A trained, slightly rehearsed quality at the door
- Genuine belief — they are not pretending
- Variable confidence: experienced publishers are smoother, newer publishers stumble
- Difference between **on-script** (door interaction) and **off-script** (real moments)

When writing player-side dialogue, give the player **at least one choice that breaks script** in each conversation — an option to say something genuine, awkward, or unexpected. This is where the doubt mechanic will eventually live. For M3 these options exist but don't yet gate.

## 8. Tone Red Lines

**Do not:**
- Write JW characters as stupid, brainwashed, evil, or self-aware-of-being-in-a-cult. They are sincere.
- Write householders as strawmen. The Polite Refuser is not stupid for refusing; the Curious Seeker is not naïve for engaging.
- Write on-the-nose theological debates. Real door interactions almost never reach actual theology.
- Deploy mockery of belief itself. Critique is delivered through showing specific behaviors, never narration.
- Emotionally manipulate the player toward a verdict. Present, don't preach.
- Use horror or thriller tone. This is observational realism.
- Reference real organizations, publications, songs, or living leaders.

**Do:**
- Trust the player to notice
- Lean into mundane texture (waiting in a parked car, a tract dropped on a porch in the rain)
- Allow JW characters dignity, warmth, kindness, and real love for each other
- Allow householders their full humanity in brief moments
- Allow silence

---

## 9. Pacing Norms

- **Door interaction full duration:** 15 seconds to 3 minutes in real life. In-game, 3-7 dialogue exchanges per GDD § 6.
- **Choice density:** Player gets a meaningful choice every 1-2 exchanges, not every line.
- **Beat after refusal:** When the conversation ends, allow a half-second of silence before scene exit. The walk back to the sidewalk matters.

## 10. Texture Notes

Specific lived-experience details. Dialogue agents should weave these in where natural, not as exposition.

### Field service mornings
- Gathering at a member's home before going out: coffee, a brief prayer, a song from *Songs of the Truth*, a short discussion of the day's "presentation" (the planned magazine offer or scripture).
- The service overseer or group leader assigns partners. Pairings carry social weight — who got paired with whom is information.
- Field service bags: small canvas or leather, holding magazines, tracts, a Bible (in-game: *The New System Translation*), a notebook for return-visit details.
- Cars parked one or two streets over from the assigned territory. Walking the territory in pairs is the unit.
- A "not at home" slip is sometimes left — a small printed note saying we stopped by.

### At the door
- Both publishers stand. The one not speaking is the silent partner; they pray internally during the interaction. In-game this can manifest as the silent partner being slightly out of focus.
- The publisher carries the magazine pre-folded to the article they're presenting.
- Eye contact is steady. Smiling is trained.
- The pitch is calibrated by the householder's reaction: if they soften, the publisher slows down. If they tense, the publisher accelerates to the magazine offer.

### Inside the congregation
- "We love you" lands differently before bad news. When an elder says it, brace.
- "Can I ask you something?" from a peer is casual. From an elder, it isn't.
- "Encouraging" is the verb used for what is functionally a reprimand. "Brother Phillips encouraged me about my dress at the convention."
- The phrase "the brothers" in passive voice signals an elder body decision: "The brothers felt it would be best if..." No agent named.
- Walking into the Hall late carries weight. People notice. No one says anything.
- The Watchtower study: members are called on to "comment" on prepared questions. Coming unprepared and being called on is a small public humiliation.
- After the meeting, lingering in the lobby is the social hour. Who talks to whom is the week's data.

### Family worship
- A scheduled weekly evening (usually Monday or Tuesday off the meeting nights) for family-led study.
- Tone is performative-earnest. Children may be quizzed.
- The phrase "What did you learn?" carries a specific weight.

### Holidays
- The studied non-celebration of Christmas, Easter, birthdays. School pickups on birthdays are awkward — the JW kid waits outside the classroom while cupcakes are passed around.
- The "alternative" — a "just because" gift later in the week, never on the day.

### Prayer cadence
- Public prayer opens with "Heavenly Father Jehovah" and closes with "we ask in Jesus' name. Amen."
- Prayers reference current events, congregation members by name, and end-times hope.
- A long prayer is a status signal. Praying well in public is a male-coded skill in the org.

### Disfellowshipping
- The announcement is two sentences at the end of a meeting: "[Name] is no longer one of Jehovah's Witnesses." No reason given.
- The room is silent. No one looks at the family.
- The shunning begins the next day. It is total. Family members are expected to participate.
- People sometimes nod or smile at a disfellowshipped person they pass on the sidewalk — a small private resistance. Then they don't.

### Leaving
- "Fading" — quietly stopping attendance without formal departure. Most who leave do this.
- The phone stops ringing within a few weeks.
- Cards stop arriving.
- Someone you considered close walks past you at a grocery store.

## 11. When You're Unsure

If the dialogue subagent is unsure whether a specific detail is right, **leave a comment in the generated dialogue file**:

```
# UNSURE: would a Polite Refuser actually use the phrase "all set"
# in this region/age? Andrew to verify.
```

Better to flag than to ship a generic line. Andrew will catch off-notes faster reading a flagged draft than reading clean prose.
